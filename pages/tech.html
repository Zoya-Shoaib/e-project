Advanced Harvesting Techniques

Explore innovative methods for collecting and managing rainwater to promote sustainability, reduce water waste, and support eco-friendly practices in urban and rural environments.

Rooftop Harvesting

Rooftop harvesting is a simple,Capturing pure rainwater directly from your building’s primary catchment surface, such as roofs, using gutters and downspouts to channel water into storage tanks for reuse in daily activities like washing or irrigation.

Rooftop harvesting captures rainwater from roofs using gutters and downpipes to store it for tasks like washing or irrigation. Adding a simple filter and diverter keeps the water clean and helps recharge groundwater levels.

Surface Runoff

Collecting and treating water from paved surfaces like roads and parking lots, where runoff is directed through drains and filters to remove contaminants, making it suitable for non-potable uses such as landscape irrigation and reducing flood risks.

Surface runoff harvesting involves collecting rainwater from paved areas like roads and parking lots. The water is directed through drains and filters to remove contaminants, making it safe for non-potable uses like landscaping. This method helps reduce local flood risks while providing an alternative water source for irrigation.

Recharge Pits

Replenishing groundwater tables through engineered percolation systems, where rainwater is directed into excavated pits lined with permeable materials to allow slow infiltration, helping maintain aquifer levels and prevent soil erosion.

Recharge pits are designed to replenish groundwater by directing filtered rainwater directly into the earth. Instead of storing water in tanks, this technique allows it to seep into the soil, helping to raise the natural water table. It is a highly effective way to prevent water waste and ensure long-term water security in urban areas.

Check Dams

Constructing small, temporary barriers across streams or gullies to slow down water flow, allowing sedimentation and percolation into the ground, which helps recharge groundwater, reduce soil erosion, and prevent downstream flooding in arid or hilly regions.

Check dams are small, temporary barriers built across streams or gullies to slow down water flow. This delay allows for sedimentation and lets water percolate into the ground, which recharges groundwater and prevents soil erosion.

Percolation Tanks

Building small reservoirs or tanks in natural depressions to collect and store rainwater, allowing it to percolate slowly into the soil and replenish groundwater aquifers, while also supporting irrigation, reducing runoff, and enhancing water availability.

Percolation tanks are small reservoirs built in natural depressions to collect rainwater and allow it to seep slowly into the soil. This process replenishes underground aquifers and provides a reliable water source for irrigation during droughts.

Trenches

Excavating shallow trenches along contours or slopes to capture and direct rainwater, promoting infiltration into the soil to recharge groundwater, control erosion, and improve soil moisture for agriculture, especially in rural landscapes with low permeability.

Trenches are shallow channels excavated along slopes or contours to capture rainwater and promote soil infiltration. They are especially useful in rural areas to recharge groundwater, control erosion, and improve soil moisture for agriculture.
